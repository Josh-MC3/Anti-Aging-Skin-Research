## How Skin Ages, Briefly

Visible aging comes from two processes happening at once. **Intrinsic aging** is the slow, chronological process driven by genetics — it happens even on skin that's never seen the sun. **Extrinsic aging** is driven by environment, and since UV exposure is the dominant factor, it's usually just called **photoaging**. Of the two, extrinsic aging tends to produce the more dramatic visible changes — and it's also the one with the most room for prevention. (Full mechanism: *The Science of Aging*.)

Underneath both processes, a handful of cellular mechanisms do most of the work:

- **Collagen and elastin** are the dermis's main structural proteins — collagen for firmness, elastin for recoil. Both decline with age, and UV exposure actively accelerates the breakdown of both.
- **Hyaluronic acid** is a natural humectant in the dermis that holds many times its weight in water, keeping skin hydrated and plump; it also declines with age.
- **Glycation** is a sugar-driven process where sugars bind to proteins like collagen, making them stiffer and harder for the body to renew.
- **Oxidative stress** happens when reactive molecules ("free radicals") — generated by UV, pollution, and normal metabolism — outpace skin's antioxidant defenses, damaging collagen, elastin, and even cellular DNA.
- **Inflammation**, whether from sun exposure, irritation, or other triggers, activates pathways that compound the damage from the mechanisms above rather than acting as a separate, isolated process.
- **Hormonal changes**, especially the estrogen decline of menopause, measurably affect collagen production and skin thickness.

These mechanisms interact constantly rather than operating independently — which is part of why no single ingredient or product addresses everything skin aging involves. (Full depth on each: *The Science of Aging*.)

## How This Site Rates Evidence

Every intervention on this site — an ingredient, a device, a procedure, a habit — gets evaluated against a tiered evidence hierarchy (clinical guidelines and meta-analyses at the top; marketing claims and anecdotes at the bottom) and rated using a consistent ★ system. That ★ rating always means **evidence quality** specifically. Effect size, safety, and cost are shown as separate ratings alongside it — a real, well-documented small effect looks different from an unproven large claim, and the two are never collapsed into one number.

This sometimes produces results that cut against popular expectation. Across this site so far: **daily sunscreen has the single strongest piece of intervention evidence on the entire site** — a real randomized controlled trial, stronger than the evidence behind any topical ingredient, device, or procedure reviewed. Meanwhile some heavily-marketed categories (certain devices, certain supplements, certain procedures) turn out to have considerably thinner independent evidence than their popularity would suggest. This isn't a contrarian stance for its own sake — it's just what happens when popularity and evidence strength, which aren't the same thing, are checked against each other directly.

## Common Misconceptions

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** The most expensive or most talked-about products and procedures are generally the most effective.
> **Fact:** Price and popularity don't reliably track with evidence quality on this site. Some of the strongest-evidenced interventions reviewed (daily sunscreen, topical retinoids) are inexpensive and unglamorous; some of the most heavily marketed categories (certain devices, certain "regenerative" ingredients) currently rest on considerably thinner independent evidence.

## Clinical Bottom Line

Skin aging is driven by genetics (intrinsic aging) and environment (extrinsic aging, overwhelmingly UV-driven), acting through interacting mechanisms — collagen/elastin breakdown, hyaluronic acid decline, glycation, oxidative stress, inflammation, and hormonal change. This site evaluates every intervention against that mechanistic backdrop and a consistent evidence-tier system, rating evidence quality separately from effect size, safety, and cost — so readers can tell the difference between "well-proven but modest" and "popular but unproven," which are very different things that get blurred together everywhere else online.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Where should I actually start if I'm completely new to this?**
Use the decision tool above, or jump directly to *Skin Through the Decades* for an age-banded starting point, or *Build Your Routine* for a structured, tiered routine-builder.

**Is this site selling anything?**
No — recommendations are based on independent clinical evidence, not sponsorship. Where a cited study is industry-funded, that's disclosed directly in the relevant page's references.

**Why does this site sometimes rate a popular product or procedure lower than expected?**
Because popularity and evidence strength are genuinely different things, and this site rates by the latter. Several pages document this pattern directly (see Device Therapy on microcurrent, Cosmetic Procedures on thread lifts, Nutrition & Supplements on zinc) — popularity doesn't reliably predict how strong the underlying clinical evidence actually is.

**How often is this site updated?**
Each page lists a last-reviewed context in its references; evidence in this field evolves, and pages are written to be revisited as new research is published rather than treated as permanently settled.
