## Overview

This page covers the evidence, cost, longevity, and risk profile of the major injectable, energy-based, and surgical cosmetic procedures: botulinum toxin (Botox and similar), dermal fillers, chemical peels, PRP, fat grafting, thread lifts, facelifts, and body contouring/skin removal surgery. This is the most procedurally serious page on the site, and the evidence quality varies more here than almost anywhere else — from genuinely rigorous, large RCTs (botulinum toxin) to techniques a peer-reviewed systematic review itself describes as "scarcely studied" (thread lifts).

> **Quick Takeaway**
> - **Botulinum toxin (Botox and similar) has the strongest, most rigorous evidence of any procedure on this page** — multiple Level 1 RCTs, FDA on-label duration data, five approved formulations.
> - **Hyaluronic acid fillers are broadly safe and effective for volume**, but a genuine meta-analysis of 5 RCTs (748 participants) found no significant difference in aesthetic improvement scores at several measured timepoints despite a higher overall responder rate than control — a real example of mixed precision worth understanding before assuming uniform benefit.
> - **PDO thread lifts are explicitly described in peer-reviewed literature as "a scarcely studied" technique** — real positive case series exist, but the rigorous comparative evidence base most other procedures on this page have is largely absent.
> - **PRP's own systematic review describes the evidence as limited, with highly varied results and ill-defined application methods** — promising, biologically plausible, but not yet a settled, standardized treatment.

## Botulinum Toxin (Botox, Dysport, Xeomin, and Others)

### Evidence — ★★★★★ Strong Evidence

This is the best-evidenced procedure on this page by a wide margin. Botulinum toxin injections are the most commonly performed minimally invasive cosmetic procedure in the US, with nearly 4 million procedures performed in 2022 alone.[1] Five botulinum toxin type A formulations are FDA-approved specifically for temporary improvement of glabellar lines, and on-label FDA duration-of-effect claims range from 3 to 4 months, with cosmetic benefit evident within days and peak effect typically reached within 30 days.[1] A Phase 2, randomized, double-blind, active-controlled trial found median duration of effect around 148-183 days depending on formulation and dose — Level of Evidence 1, the strongest rating in the clinical evidence hierarchy.[1]

Mechanistically, botulinum toxin inhibits acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction, temporarily preventing the small, repeated muscle contractions that create dynamic wrinkles (the kind that form from expression, as opposed to wrinkles from collagen/elastin loss alone — see *The Science of Aging*).[2]

### Who Benefits / Who Should Avoid

Best suited to dynamic wrinkles (crow's feet, glabellar lines, forehead lines) caused by repeated muscle movement — it does not address static wrinkles caused by structural collagen/elastin loss or volume loss, which require different approaches (fillers, energy-based devices, or surgery). Standard contraindications include pregnancy/breastfeeding, certain neuromuscular conditions, and known allergy to the formulation.

### Costs / Side Effects

Requires repeat treatment every 3-4 months to maintain effect, which compounds cost over time relative to a one-time procedure — directly relevant to the *Cost vs Benefit Calculator*. Documented side effects include localized bruising, and in eye-area treatment specifically, risks include diplopia, ptosis (eyelid drooping), and lagophthalmos, generally transient.[3]

## Dermal Fillers (Hyaluronic Acid-Based)

### Evidence — ★★★★ Moderate Evidence — with a genuinely important precision caveat

Hyaluronic acid (HA) fillers have a large and generally favorable evidence base for facial volumization, with the best data available for nasolabial folds and the periocular region.[4] HA fillers are temporary, gradually broken down by the body (typically lasting 6-9 months, though longevity varies meaningfully by product, injection site, and technique), and are reversible via hyaluronidase if needed — a genuine safety advantage over permanent filler materials.[4]

This page wants to highlight a specific, important finding rather than presenting filler evidence as uniformly strong: a systematic review and meta-analysis of 5 RCTs (748 participants) found HA fillers produced a significantly higher *responder rate* than control, but **Global Aesthetic Improvement Scale scores showed no statistically significant improvement at 4, 8, or 24 weeks** in the same analysis.[5] This is a genuinely useful example of how two different ways of measuring the same outcome (responder rate vs. continuous improvement score) can tell different stories about the same body of evidence — neither figure is wrong, but citing only the more favorable one would overstate the precision of what's actually been shown.

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** All hyaluronic acid fillers have equally strong, directly comparable clinical evidence behind them.
> **Fact:** A primary clinical trial publication itself notes that despite progress in filler design, there is still limited evidence on the clinical efficacy of many specific products, and comparative studies between different fillers are often lacking — meaning clinicians (and patients) frequently rely on manufacturer-provided information rather than independent comparative data when choosing between specific filler products.[6]

### Who Benefits / Who Should Avoid

Good candidates have isolated volume loss or static wrinkles in specific areas (nasolabial folds, cheeks, lips, tear troughs) where the evidence base is strongest. Serious complications are rare but real: vascular occlusion and skin necrosis are documented, rare risks requiring prompt recognition and treatment by an experienced injector.[5]

### Costs

Requires repeat treatment as the filler is gradually absorbed (most commonly 6-9 months, with real variation by product and area); cumulative cost over years should be weighed against more durable options on the *Cost vs Benefit Calculator*.

## Chemical Peels

### Evidence — ★★★★ Moderate Evidence (superficial/medium peels); evidence varies meaningfully by depth

Chemical peels (using AHAs, BHAs, or stronger agents like TCA or phenol at deeper concentrations) work through controlled chemical exfoliation, with depth of peel directly correlated to both effect size and recovery time/risk. Superficial peels (using ingredients overlapping with *Skincare Ingredients*' AHA/BHA evidence) have a solid evidence base for texture and mild pigmentation; deeper peels have stronger single-treatment effects but meaningfully higher risk of complications including, notably, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation in melanin-rich skin (see *Age, Genetics & Skin Type* for the broader skin-of-color procedure-selection discussion) and, rarely, scarring.

### Who Benefits / Who Should Avoid

Peel depth should be individually selected based on Fitzpatrick type and specific concern — this is a procedure where the skin-type-specific risk considerations discussed elsewhere on this site are directly actionable.

## Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP)

### Evidence — ★★★ Emerging Evidence

PRP uses a concentrated portion of the patient's own blood plasma (containing platelets at 150,000-400,000/µL, several times normal concentration) injected to stimulate tissue regeneration via released growth factors.[7] A systematic review of PRP for facial rejuvenation (9 clinical trials, 2 observational studies) found that 4 of the included studies indicated improved wrinkles and skin texture, with histological evidence of increased dermal density.[7] However, the same review is direct about a real limitation: evidence on PRP's effects is limited, results are highly varied, and application methods are ill-defined across the existing literature.[7]

This is a case where genuine biological plausibility (it's autologous, so allergic/rejection risk is low, and growth-factor-mediated tissue stimulation is a real mechanism) coexists with a literature that hasn't yet standardized how PRP should be prepared, dosed, or administered for cosmetic use — meaning two patients receiving "PRP treatment" at different clinics may be receiving meaningfully different actual interventions.

### Who Benefits / Who Should Avoid

Low allergy/rejection risk given its autologous nature; the main current limitation is inconsistent technique and preparation standards across providers rather than a specific contraindication profile.

## Thread Lifts (PDO, PLLA, PCL)

### Evidence — ★★ Weak Evidence — explicitly described as understudied in the peer-reviewed literature itself

This page wants to be direct about thread lifts specifically, since they're frequently marketed as a "non-surgical facelift" with confidence that outpaces the literature. A 2023 systematic review and case report on PDO threads is titled, in the authors' own words, "a scarcely studied rejuvenation technique," and explicitly notes that despite gaining popularity among plastic surgeons and dermatologists, there have been very few published scientific studies on the effects of absorbable threads in facial rejuvenation.[8]

That said, the studies that do exist generally report positive results: one 2-year outcome study found significant facial rejuvenation with barbed PDO threads, particularly when combined with Botox, fillers, and/or PRP, and a separate prospective study of 68 patients found clinically significant facial lifting lasting an average of 18 months.[8][9] Newer thread materials (PCL) show evidence of generating more collagen and longer tissue retention than PDO in comparative research, though this comparative evidence base is itself still developing.[8]

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** Thread lifts are a well-established, thoroughly studied alternative to surgical facelifts with a comparable evidence base.
> **Fact:** A peer-reviewed systematic review of this exact technique describes it in its own title as "a scarcely studied rejuvenation technique." Positive results exist in the available studies, but the volume and rigor of independent comparative research is genuinely thinner than for botulinum toxin or HA fillers — this page rates it accordingly rather than assuming popularity reflects evidence strength.

### Who Benefits / Who Should Avoid

Best suited to mild-moderate laxity given the temporary/absorbable nature of the threads; documented risks include thread migration, skin puckering/dimpling, and — per some sources reviewed — potential scar tissue formation that could complicate future procedures, a consideration worth discussing directly with a provider given the comparatively thin evidence base.[10]

## Fat Grafting

### Evidence — ★★★ Emerging Evidence

Fat grafting (autologous fat transfer) uses the patient's own harvested fat to restore volume, offering a biological, potentially longer-lasting alternative to synthetic fillers in appropriate candidates. Evidence quality varies by technique and harvest/processing method; this remains an area of active surgical refinement rather than a single standardized procedure with one uniform evidence base.

## Facelifts & Surgical Procedures

### Evidence — ★★★★ Moderate-to-Strong Evidence (for surgical outcomes); longest track record on this page

Surgical facelift remains the most durable solution for significant skin laxity and structural aging change, with decades of surgical refinement and outcome data, though — consistent with surgical literature generally — much of the evidence base is observational/cohort rather than RCT-structured, since randomizing patients to surgery versus no surgery for cosmetic indications is both impractical and not typically pursued. This is the appropriate escalation point when topical, device, and minimally-invasive injectable approaches have been tried and structural change (rather than just surface texture or fine lines) is the primary concern.

### Who Benefits / Who Should Avoid

Best candidates have significant skin laxity and structural change rather than fine lines or volume loss alone (which are better addressed by less invasive options higher up this page). This carries the highest risk profile and longest recovery of any procedure on this page, requiring the most thorough in-person evaluation of any option discussed.

## Body Contouring & Skin Removal Surgery

Covered in direct connection with *Exercise & Body Composition*'s loose-skin discussion: body contouring (abdominoplasty, brachioplasty, and similar procedures) is the direct surgical solution for excess skin following significant weight loss that exercise and topical approaches cannot resolve. As discussed there, this carries real quality-of-life benefit for functionally significant excess skin, though post-bariatric body contouring patients show measurably lower quality-of-life scores than the general population even after successful surgery — a sobering, honest finding worth knowing going in, not a reason to avoid a procedure that meaningfully helps the specific problem it targets, but a reminder that surgery addresses the skin itself, not necessarily every aspect of the broader experience of significant weight change.[11]

## Who Benefits / Who Should Avoid (General)

Across every procedure on this page: pregnancy/breastfeeding is a standard contraindication pending specific guidance; certain blood thinners and bleeding disorders require special precaution and disclosure; and realistic expectations matter most for the categories with thinner evidence (thread lifts, PRP) where individual results plausibly vary more than the available literature can yet precisely characterize.

## Common Misconceptions

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** Newer, more expensive, or more heavily marketed procedures are automatically more effective than established options.
> **Fact:** Botulinum toxin — among the oldest, least expensive procedures on this page — has the strongest evidence base by a wide margin. Several newer techniques (thread lifts, some PRP applications) currently have thinner independent evidence than their marketing presence would suggest.

---

## Research Gaps

- **Thread lifts** are the clearest stated research gap on this page, by the field's own peer-reviewed admission.
- **PRP standardization** — preparation method, platelet concentration, and injection technique all vary across the existing literature, making it hard to compare results across studies or know which specific protocol a given study's findings apply to.
- **Comparative effectiveness research between different HA filler products** is limited, per a primary trial's own stated limitation — most decisions currently rest on manufacturer-provided data rather than independent head-to-head comparison.

## Clinical Bottom Line

Botulinum toxin has by far the strongest evidence base on this page and is the most rigorously studied minimally-invasive cosmetic procedure available. Hyaluronic acid fillers are broadly safe and effective for facial volumization, though readers should know that aesthetic improvement *scores* specifically (as opposed to overall responder rates) haven't always shown statistical significance in rigorous meta-analysis — a precision distinction worth understanding rather than assuming uniform, unambiguous benefit. PRP is biologically plausible and shows real promise but remains methodologically unstandardized. Thread lifts are explicitly, by the field's own peer-reviewed literature, an understudied technique — positive results exist, but the evidence base is genuinely thinner than its popularity suggests. Surgical procedures (facelift, body contouring) remain the most durable options for significant structural change, with the highest risk and recovery burden to match. Every procedure on this page requires an in-person consultation with a licensed provider — nothing here substitutes for that conversation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is Botox safe long-term?**
It has the longest and most rigorous safety track record of any procedure on this page, with decades of use and multiple FDA-approved formulations; standard medical contraindications and provider guidance still apply.

**Are fillers or thread lifts better for sagging skin?**
Neither directly addresses true structural sagging the way a surgical procedure does — fillers restore volume, threads provide temporary, evidence-thin lift. For significant structural laxity, a consultation about surgical options is the most evidence-grounded next step.

**Is PRP worth trying?**
It's a reasonable, low-risk option to discuss with a provider given its biological plausibility and autologous safety profile, but go in understanding that the field hasn't yet standardized exactly how it should be prepared or administered — ask your specific provider about their protocol and its basis.

**Why does this page rate thread lifts so much lower than I expected given how popular they are?**
Because popularity and evidence strength are different things, and this page rates by the latter — the relevant systematic review literally titles itself around how understudied the technique is, which this page takes at face value rather than assuming the procedure's popularity reflects equivalent evidence.
