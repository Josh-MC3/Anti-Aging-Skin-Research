## The Evidence Rating System

Per *Phase 2*'s framework, every intervention reviewed on this site is rated on **multiple separate axes** — never blended into a single combined score. This is the most important thing to understand about how ratings work on this site.

### Evidence Quality (the ★ rating shown throughout)

| Rating | Criteria |
|---|---|
| ★★★★★ Strong Evidence | Guideline-level or meta-analysis/systematic-review support, consistent across multiple independent, adequately powered studies. |
| ★★★★ Moderate Evidence | Multiple randomized controlled trials, generally consistent, reasonable sample sizes. |
| ★★★ Emerging Evidence | A small number of RCTs or good cohort data; promising but not yet widely replicated, or mixed results. |
| ★★ Weak Evidence | Mostly case-control, cross-sectional, or mechanistic/animal/lab data; human clinical data sparse. |
| ★ Minimal Evidence | Expert opinion or marketing/anecdote only, or contradicted by what limited evidence exists. |

**This rating always means evidence quality specifically** — never effect size, safety, or cost. Those appear as separate, adjacent ratings:

- **Magnitude of Effect:** Large / Moderate / Small / Negligible-Unclear — how big the effect is, independent of how well-proven it is.
- **Safety:** Generally well-tolerated / Moderate caution / Significant caution — independent of how well the intervention works.
- **Consistency of Evidence:** Highly consistent / Mostly consistent / Inconsistent — how much studies on the same question agree with each other.
- **Long-Term Data:** Available / Limited / Absent — whether evidence extends beyond 6-12 months.

**Why this matters:** A intervention can be ★★★★★ (strong evidence) for a small effect (e.g., topical caffeine's well-studied but modest effect) or ★★ (weak evidence) for a claim of large effect (e.g., some growth-factor serum marketing). Collapsing these into one score would hide exactly the distinction this site is built to preserve.

## The Evidence Hierarchy (Source Tiers)

Used throughout every page's References section, from strongest to weakest:

| Tier | Source Type |
|---|---|
| 1 | Clinical practice guidelines, professional society guidelines, government health agencies |
| 2 | Umbrella reviews, meta-analyses, systematic reviews |
| 3 | Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) |
| 4 | Prospective cohort studies |
| 5 | Case-control studies |
| 6 | Cross-sectional studies |
| 7 | Mechanistic, animal, or laboratory studies |
| 8 | Expert opinion (including dermatologist-authored clinical-education content) |
| 9 | Marketing claims, influencer claims, anecdotal reports |

A single Tier 1-2 source generally outweighs multiple Tier 7-9 sources — this site does not "average" evidence quality across tiers.

## Glossary of Recurring Terms

**AGEs (Advanced Glycation End-Products)** — Compounds formed when sugars bind to proteins like collagen without enzymatic help, making the protein stiffer and harder for the body to renew. See *The Science of Aging*.

**Cellular senescence** — A state where cells permanently stop dividing but don't die, instead secreting inflammatory signals that damage surrounding tissue. Driven by telomere shortening and mitochondrial dysfunction. See *The Science of Aging*.

**Collagen** — The dermis's main structural protein, responsible for skin firmness. Declines with both intrinsic (chronological) and extrinsic (UV-driven) aging. See *The Science of Aging*.

**Elastin** — The protein responsible for skin's recoil/elasticity. Under UV exposure, elastin production increases but becomes disorganized (see *solar elastosis* below). See *The Science of Aging*.

**Extrinsic aging (photoaging)** — Aging caused by environmental factors, overwhelmingly UV exposure. Tends to produce more dramatic visible change than intrinsic aging alone. See *The Science of Aging*.

**Fitzpatrick scale** — A six-category skin classification originally designed to predict UV burn/tan response and guide phototherapy dosing — not a measure of race or ethnicity, despite documented clinical misuse in that direction. See *Age, Genetics & Skin Type*.

**Glycation** — See *AGEs* above.

**Intrinsic aging** — The natural, chronological aging process driven by genetics, occurring even on sun-protected skin. See *The Science of Aging*.

**MED (Minimal Erythemal Dose)** — The lowest UV dose that produces detectable sunburn 24 hours later; used as a research measure of photoprotection (e.g., in omega-3 supplementation studies). See *Nutrition & Supplements*.

**Melanocyte** — The cell responsible for producing melanin (pigment). Becomes hyperactive in melasma and activated as a protective response in PIH. See *Hyperpigmentation*.

**MMPs (Matrix Metalloproteinases)** — Enzymes activated by UV exposure that actively degrade existing collagen while suppressing new collagen production. A primary mechanism many well-evidenced skincare actives (retinoids, vitamin C) work by inhibiting. See *The Science of Aging*, *Skincare Ingredients*.

**PIH (Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation)** — Localized dark marks resulting from a specific prior inflammatory event (acne, injury, irritation), distinct from melasma's ongoing hormonal/light-driven process. See *Hyperpigmentation*.

**RCT (Randomized Controlled Trial)** — A study design where participants are randomly assigned to receive an intervention or a control/comparator, minimizing bias in comparing outcomes. Tier 3 on this site's evidence hierarchy.

**Solar elastosis** — The microscopic hallmark of photoaging: a disorganized buildup of damaged elastic fibers in the upper-to-mid dermis. See *The Science of Aging*.

**Tretinoin** — The prescription-strength retinoid (vitamin A derivative); the most rigorously evidenced topical anti-aging active reviewed on this site. See *Skincare Ingredients*.

## Common Misconceptions

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** A single ★ rating can tell you everything you need to know about whether an intervention is "worth it."
> **Fact:** Evidence quality (★) is one of several axes this site tracks — magnitude of effect, safety, cost, and consistency are reported separately precisely because they answer different questions. A reader who only looks at the star count misses most of what this site is trying to communicate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why isn't there a single combined "score" for each intervention?**
Because blending evidence quality with effect size, safety, and cost would hide exactly the distinctions this site is built to preserve — e.g., a well-proven small effect should look different from an unproven large claim. See the Evidence Rating System section above.

**What does it mean if a page cites a "Tier 8" source?**
Tier 8 means expert opinion or clinical-education content — useful and often appropriate for mechanism explanations or practical synthesis, but a different (lower) evidence standard than a systematic review or RCT. Several pages flag explicitly when their citation mix leans more heavily on Tier 8 than usual.

**Is this page itself evidence-based?**
This page doesn't make clinical claims of its own — it's a glossary and rating-key reference, consolidating definitions already established and cited on the content pages it links to.
