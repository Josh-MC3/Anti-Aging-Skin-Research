## Overview

Several pages on this site (*Cosmetic Procedures*, *Device Therapy*, *Nutrition & Supplements*) explicitly point here for a direct cost-to-evidence comparison. This page delivers that: every intervention reviewed across the site, placed on one consistent framework — evidence quality, expected improvement, annual cost, time commitment, and maintenance burden — so "does it work" and "is it worth it" can be answered together rather than separately.

> **Quick Takeaway**
> - **"Strong evidence" and "worth the money" are different questions, and this page answers both at once.** Botulinum toxin has the strongest procedural evidence on the site but requires recurring cost every 3-4 months indefinitely; daily sunscreen has comparably strong evidence at a small fraction of the cost, with no maintenance escalation over time.
> - **By annualized cost-to-evidence ratio, the top of this ranking is dominated by inexpensive, unglamorous interventions** — sunscreen and topical retinoids — not devices or procedures.
> - **High cost does not predict high evidence tier anywhere on this site.** Some of the most expensive categories reviewed (LED devices, certain procedures) sit in the middle of the evidence-quality range, not the top.

## The ROI Framework

Per *Phase 2*'s evidence-rating system, this page does not blend evidence quality into a single combined score with cost and effect size — each stays a separate, visible axis, consistent with every other page on this site. What this page adds is **annualized cost**, since comparing a one-time procedure's sticker price against a recurring topical's monthly cost is misleading without putting both on the same yearly basis.

| Tier | Annual Cost Band (USD, approximate) |
|---|---|
| Low | Under $150/year |
| Moderate | $150-600/year |
| High | $600-2,000/year |
| Very High | $2,000+/year (or large one-time cost amortized) |

## Ranked by Annualized Cost-to-Evidence Ratio

This ranking weighs evidence quality and annual cost together — it is **not** a ranking of which intervention has the largest absolute effect (HIFU and facelifts produce more dramatic individual change than sunscreen, but cost vastly more per unit of independently-verified evidence).

1. **Daily sunscreen** — strongest evidence on the entire site, lowest cost, no escalation over time. The single best cost-to-evidence ratio reviewed anywhere on this site.
2. **Topical retinoid** — comparably strong evidence, low cost, though requires the patience of an adjustment period (see *Skincare Ingredients*).
3. **Niacinamide / Vitamin C** — moderate evidence, very low cost, narrower specific benefits than retinoids.
4. **Resistance training** — emerging but genuine RCT evidence for a skin-specific benefit, effectively free if bodyweight training is used.
5. **Tranexamic acid (for pigmentation specifically)** — strong, targeted evidence for melasma; not a general anti-aging pick.
6. **Botulinum toxin** — the strongest procedural evidence on the site, but the recurring cost (every 3-4 months, indefinitely) means its lifetime cost is substantially higher than its per-treatment evidence quality alone would suggest.
7. **Red/NIR light devices** — genuinely independent RCT evidence, but a meaningfully higher entry cost than any topical above it.
8. **HA fillers, RF microneedling** — real, moderate evidence, with real recurring cost and (per the relevant pages) genuine precision/funding caveats worth knowing before paying.
9. **HIFU, facelift/surgical** — real and in facelift's case strong evidence, but the highest absolute costs on this page; facelift's cost amortizes better over years than HIFU's repeat-session model.
10. **Microcurrent, thread lifts, PRP** — the weakest cost-to-evidence ratios on this page; not because they're necessarily worthless, but because their evidence tier doesn't currently justify their price point relative to options higher on this list.

## Common Misconceptions

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** More expensive interventions generally have stronger evidence behind them, since providers and manufacturers wouldn't charge a premium otherwise.
> **Fact:** The data on this page directly contradicts that assumption. The single strongest evidence on the entire site (daily sunscreen) is also among the cheapest interventions reviewed; some of the most expensive categories (HIFU, thread lifts) sit in the middle or bottom of the evidence-quality range.

---

## Research Gaps

This page's central limitation is that **"expected effect" isn't always measured the same way across interventions** being compared — a topical's effect is typically measured via validated scoring or imaging over weeks-to-months, while a procedure's effect is sometimes investigator-rated immediately post-treatment (see *Device Therapy*'s HIFU investigator-vs-patient gap for a direct example of why this matters). This page presents the comparison as honestly as the underlying evidence allows, but the "Expected Effect" column is necessarily a simplification of each page's fuller, more nuanced discussion.

---

## Clinical Bottom Line

When evidence quality and annualized cost are placed on the same comparison, the highest-ROI interventions on this entire site are inexpensive, unglamorous daily habits — sunscreen first, topical retinoids close behind — not the devices or procedures that typically receive the most marketing attention and consumer interest. This doesn't mean procedures and devices are poor choices; several (botulinum toxin, HIFU, facelift) have real, substantial evidence for effects topicals genuinely cannot achieve. But their cost-to-evidence ratio is different, and recurring procedures in particular (Botox, fillers) carry a lifetime cost that's easy to underestimate when only considering one treatment at a time.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is it ever worth paying for an expensive procedure if a cheap topical has "stronger" evidence?**
Yes — evidence quality and what an intervention is mechanistically capable of achieving are different things. No topical addresses dynamic wrinkles the way botulinum toxin does, or structural volume loss the way a filler or facelift does. This page ranks cost-to-evidence ratio, not "which single intervention is best" — see each intervention's full page for what it can and cannot mechanistically do.

**Why does sunscreen rank so much higher than Botox if Botox has equally strong evidence?**
Both have ★★★★★ evidence, but Botox requires repeat treatment every 3-4 months indefinitely, while sunscreen is a low, flat daily cost with no escalation — the same evidence tier at a very different lifetime cost changes the ROI ranking even though it doesn't change which one "works."

**Should I stop using [low-ranked intervention] because it ranks at the bottom of this page?**
Not necessarily — a low cost-to-evidence ranking reflects the current state of independent research, not a definitive verdict that something doesn't work. Several lower-ranked items (PRP, thread lifts) have genuine biological plausibility; their literature just hasn't caught up to a confident ranking yet.

**Does this page account for individual results varying?**
No — this is a population-level evidence-and-cost comparison, not a personalized prediction. See *Age, Genetics & Skin Type* for why individual variation matters, and consult a provider for guidance specific to your situation before making a procedural decision.
