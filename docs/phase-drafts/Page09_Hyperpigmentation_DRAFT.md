## Overview

This page covers the major causes of skin darkening and uneven tone — melasma, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH), and friction/hormonal darkening (underarms, knees, elbows, neck) — what causes each, evidence-based treatment options (cross-referencing *Skincare Ingredients* for the relevant actives), and when a pigmentation change warrants professional evaluation rather than home treatment.

> **Quick Takeaway**
> - **Melasma, PIH, and friction/hormonal darkening are mechanistically different conditions** that get lumped together in consumer content but respond differently to treatment — getting the cause right matters more than picking a trendy ingredient.
> - **Visible light (not just UV) can trigger and worsen melasma** — a genuinely underappreciated point, since standard UVA/UVB sunscreen alone doesn't fully address it.
> - **A rigorous systematic review found a real lack of robust efficacy across PIH treatments in skin of color** — this page states that honestly rather than overpromising.
> - **Dark, velvety patches at the neck, underarms, or skin folds (acanthosis nigricans) can be a genuine marker of undiagnosed insulin resistance** — this is the clearest "see a doctor, not just a dermatologist" flag on this page.

## What's Actually Causing This? (Triage by Pattern)

Before treatment, it helps to identify which category a pigmentation concern likely falls into, since the evidence-based approach differs meaningfully by cause:

| Pattern | Likely Cause | Key Distinguishing Feature |
|---|---|---|
| Symmetrical brown patches on cheeks/forehead/upper lip, often worse in pregnancy or with hormonal contraceptives | Melasma | Symmetrical, sun/visible-light-triggered, hormonally influenced |
| Flat dark marks exactly where a pimple, cut, bite, or rash was | Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH) | Traceable to a specific prior injury/inflammation event |
| Darkening in skin folds (neck, underarms, groin), velvety/thickened texture | Friction-related or acanthosis nigricans | Texture change (thickened, velvety), not just color |
| Isolated, asymmetric, changing, or bleeding spot | **Not covered by this page's home-treatment guidance** | See "When to See a Professional" below |

## Melasma

### Mechanism

Melasma's pathogenesis is multifactorial and genuinely not fully understood, but three drivers are well established: **UV/visible light exposure**, **hormonal influence**, and **genetic predisposition**.[1] Melanocytes in melasma-affected skin become hyperactive, producing excessive melanin even without significant new UV exposure — meaning melasma patches can persist or worsen even with sun avoidance, because the melanocytes themselves are dysregulated, not just reacting normally to light.[2]

The hormonal connection is real and substantial: melasma occurs more frequently in women than men and commonly develops or worsens during pregnancy, supporting a role for estrogen and progesterone signaling directly in melanocyte activity.[1][3]

> **Clinical Pearl:** A genuinely underappreciated point: visible light (around 415nm, not just UV-A/UV-B) can trigger melasma-related pigmentation that persists for months.[1] Standard sunscreens that only filter UV-A and UV-B are therefore "unsatisfactory" for melasma specifically, per clinical reviews — tinted, mineral sunscreens containing iron oxide provide better visible-light protection than untinted formulations, which is a specific, actionable detail most general sun-protection advice misses.

### Evidence — Treatment

First-line treatment is strict sun/visible-light avoidance combined with broad-spectrum, high-SPF (50+) tinted sunscreen — without this, "potentially successful treatments... are doomed to fail," per clinical guidance.[1] Topical depigmenting agents are the mainstay beyond sun protection: tranexamic acid and hydroquinone both have strong head-to-head trial support (see *Skincare Ingredients* — the 5% tranexamic acid vs. 3% hydroquinone trial found comparable efficacy without hydroquinone's long-term risk). Topical retinoids and azelaic acid are also used as adjuncts.[1] Second-line options include chemical peels and laser, generally reserved for cases not responding to topical management.

## Post-Inflammatory Hyperpigmentation (PIH)

### Mechanism

PIH is fundamentally different from melasma: it's a direct, localized reaction to a specific prior inflammatory event (acne, eczema flare, insect bite, cut, burn, aggressive extraction, or even harsh skincare) rather than an ongoing hormonal/light-driven process.[4] When skin is inflamed, melanocytes are activated as a protective response and deposit excess melanin at the exact site of injury — appearing as a flat mark, sometimes long after the original inflammation has resolved.[5] PIH can be epidermal (brown/tan, responds better to topical treatment) or dermal (gray/blue-gray, slower to respond, deeper pigment deposition).[5]

PIH is more prominent and persistent in people with darker skin tones (Fitzpatrick III-VI), related to differences in melanosome size and melanin quantity, and prevalence estimates in some studied populations range from roughly 0.4% to 10%.[6]

### Evidence — Treatment: An Honest Limitation

This is a case where the evidence genuinely doesn't support the level of confidence found in most consumer content. A systematic review specifically examining PIH treatment in skin of color found **a lack of robust efficacy across all treatment modalities**, with considerable room for improvement in interventions for this population.[6] Within that review, certain agents performed somewhat better than others, but the overall picture is one of modest, inconsistent results rather than reliable, fast resolution — a meaningfully more cautious conclusion than what most PIH-focused product marketing suggests.

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** PIH can be reliably and quickly cleared with the right combination of brightening ingredients.
> **Fact:** A dedicated systematic review of treatments for PIH in skin of color found a genuine lack of robust efficacy across treatment modalities studied. PIH is real and treatable to a degree, but readers — especially those with more melanin-rich skin, where PIH is most persistent — should expect gradual, partial improvement over months rather than a fast or guaranteed result, regardless of which specific product or ingredient combination is used.

The most consistently evidence-supported approach remains: address the underlying inflammatory trigger first (treat the acne/eczema itself, stop picking/extracting), since ongoing inflammation continuously generates new pigment even while treating old marks; layer in the topical actives from *Skincare Ingredients* with the strongest pigmentation evidence (tranexamic acid, niacinamide, azelaic acid, alpha arbutin); and protect consistently from UV, since sun exposure darkens existing PIH and slows fading.

## Friction, Hormones & Skin-Fold Darkening (Underarms, Knees, Elbows, Neck)

### Mechanism — Two Distinct Categories

This is where it's especially important to separate genuinely different causes that often get marketed together as one "problem":

**1. Frictional/mechanical hyperpigmentation.** Chronic, low-grade friction (tight clothing, repetitive rubbing, shaving) is a real, distinct trigger for localized PIH — described in dermatology literature as "frictional melanosis," with one case report describing a specific pigmented pattern from repetitive nose-rubbing in allergic rhinitis, dermoscopically confirmed.[7] The mechanism is the same PIH pathway described above, just triggered by repeated mechanical irritation rather than a single inflammatory event.

**2. Acanthosis nigricans — a genuine medical marker, not just a cosmetic concern.** Dark, velvety, thickened patches at the neck, underarms, or groin can be **acanthosis nigricans**, a condition with a well-established, decades-deep evidence base linking it to insulin resistance, obesity, and type 2 diabetes.[8] This is mechanistically distinct from simple friction-based darkening: high circulating insulin levels bind insulin-like growth factor (IGF-1) receptors in skin, driving epidermal cell overgrowth and increased melanin production — a genuine metabolic signal showing up on the skin, not just a pigmentation cosmetic issue.[9] One study of 332 overweight adolescents found acanthosis nigricans presence alone predicted hyperinsulinemia and insulin resistance in a meaningful share of those screened, supporting its use as a noninvasive, cost-effective clinical screening signal.[10]

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key distinguishing feature between simple frictional darkening and acanthosis nigricans is **texture**, not just color: acanthosis nigricans patches are thickened and velvety to the touch, not just darker. If skin-fold darkening comes with a textural change — especially at the neck or underarms, with or without small skin tags nearby — this is worth raising with a physician (not just treating cosmetically), since it can be the first visible sign of undiagnosed insulin resistance or diabetes, well before other symptoms appear.

### Evidence — Treatment

For simple friction-related darkening: reducing the mechanical trigger (looser clothing, gentler shaving technique, reduced repetitive friction) plus the same PIH-appropriate topical actives discussed above. For acanthosis nigricans specifically: the evidence-based approach addresses the underlying metabolic driver (insulin resistance, weight management as appropriate, medical evaluation for diabetes) rather than topical treatment alone — topicals may modestly improve appearance but don't address the cause, and meaningful improvement typically correlates with improvement in the underlying insulin resistance.[9]

## When to See a Professional

This page's home-treatment guidance does **not** apply to:

- A pigmented spot that is new, **asymmetric**, has irregular borders, multiple colors, or is **changing in size, shape, or color**
- Any spot that **bleeds, itches persistently, or is raised/textured** in a way that wasn't present before
- **Neck/underarm/groin darkening with a velvety texture change**, especially alongside unexplained weight changes, fatigue, or other symptoms — raise this with a physician for metabolic evaluation, not just a dermatologist for cosmetic treatment
- Pigmentation changes that **don't fit cleanly into melasma, PIH, or simple friction patterns** described above

These are general triage signals, not a diagnosis — only a clinician examining the skin directly can determine what a specific spot actually is.

## Who Benefits / Who Should Avoid

Sun-protection and the topical actives discussed here are broadly appropriate for most adults. Pregnant readers with new or worsening melasma should know this is extremely common (often called "the mask of pregnancy") and frequently improves after pregnancy/breastfeeding ends and hormones stabilize — though some persistent cases do need active treatment afterward. Anyone noticing the acanthosis nigricans pattern described above should see a physician regardless of whether they pursue cosmetic treatment.

## Expected Timeline

Melasma: improvement is typically slow and incomplete without strict sun/visible-light protection; relapse is common if protection lapses. PIH: per the systematic review above, expect gradual, partial improvement over months, not fast clearing. Acanthosis nigricans: cosmetic appearance may improve as the underlying metabolic driver is addressed, on a timeline tied to that underlying treatment rather than topical skincare alone.

## Costs

Topical actives are low-to-moderate cost (see *Products Guide*); procedural escalation (peels, lasers, discussed on *Device Therapy* and *Cosmetic Procedures*) costs considerably more and, per the PIH systematic review, doesn't guarantee meaningfully better outcomes than well-executed topical care, especially in skin of color.

## Side Effects

Hydroquinone's long-term risk (ochronosis) is discussed in depth on *Skincare Ingredients*. Aggressive chemical peels or laser settings can, per several sources reviewed, actually cause "rebound" pigmentation — a real risk that argues for conservative, dermatologist-guided escalation rather than aggressive self-treatment, especially in more melanin-rich skin.

## Common Misconceptions

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** Melasma, PIH, and "dark spots" generally are all basically the same thing and respond to the same treatments.
> **Fact:** These are mechanistically distinct conditions — melasma is hormone/light-driven and chronic-relapsing; PIH is a direct reaction to a specific prior injury; friction-based darkening and acanthosis nigricans are mechanically or metabolically driven respectively. Treatment that works well for one may underperform for another.

> **Myth vs Fact**
> **Myth:** Dark, velvety patches at the neck or underarms are just a sign you need to exfoliate more or use a brightening product.
> **Fact:** This pattern (acanthosis nigricans) has a well-established evidence base linking it to insulin resistance and diabetes risk. Worth a conversation with a physician, not just a cosmetic fix.

## Research Gaps

- **PIH treatment in skin of color** is the clearest stated research gap on this page — the systematic review cited above explicitly calls for more effective interventions for this population, and this page takes that conclusion at face value rather than overselling existing options.
- **Melasma's relapse mechanism** (why it recurs even with good sun protection in some patients) remains incompletely understood — current research is still working out exactly how hormonal and light-driven pathways interact long-term.
- **Visible-light protection standards** are less standardized and less consumer-familiar than UV protection (there's no universal "visible light protection factor" equivalent to SPF), which is a real gap between what the research shows matters and what's easy for a consumer to act on.

## Clinical Bottom Line

Melasma, PIH, and friction/hormonal skin-fold darkening are mechanistically distinct and respond to different evidence-based approaches: melasma needs aggressive UV-and-visible-light protection plus topical depigmenting agents (tranexamic acid and hydroquinone have the strongest head-to-head evidence); PIH needs both treatment of the underlying inflammatory trigger and patient expectations set honestly, given a systematic review's finding of inconsistent efficacy across treatments in skin of color; friction-based darkening responds to reducing mechanical triggers plus standard pigmentation actives. Acanthosis nigricans — velvety, thickened darkening at the neck or skin folds — is the one pattern on this page that's a genuine medical signal worth a physician visit, not just a cosmetic concern.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will my melasma go away if I just stay out of the sun completely?**
Sun and visible-light avoidance is the single most important controllable factor, but melasma's hormonal and genetic components mean some patients see persistent or recurring pigmentation even with excellent sun protection — this is a chronic-relapsing condition for many people, not something that simply resolves once and stays resolved.

**Does sunscreen help with PIH the way it helps with melasma?**
Yes — sun exposure can darken existing PIH marks and slow fading, so daily sunscreen is supportive for PIH too, even though PIH itself isn't primarily sun-triggered the way melasma is.

**I have dark, slightly raised, velvety patches on my neck — is this something to worry about?**
This pattern (acanthosis nigricans) is worth mentioning to a physician, since it has a well-documented association with insulin resistance and diabetes risk — not an emergency, but a genuinely useful early signal worth checking rather than just treating cosmetically.

**Why does this page seem more cautious about PIH treatment than other sources I've seen?**
Because the most rigorous available evidence (a systematic review specifically in skin of color) found a real lack of robust efficacy across treatments studied — this page reflects that finding directly rather than the more optimistic framing common in product marketing.
