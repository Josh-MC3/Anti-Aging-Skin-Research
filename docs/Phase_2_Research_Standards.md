# Phase 2 — Evidence Standards & Research Framework

**Status:** Draft for approval. Governs every page written in Phase 4. No page content has been written yet.

---

## 1. Evidence Hierarchy

This site uses the tier system from the project's Universal Research Standards, applied consistently everywhere a claim is made:

| Tier | Evidence Type |
|------|---------------|
| 1 | Clinical Practice Guidelines · Professional Society Guidelines · Government Health Agencies |
| 2 | Umbrella Reviews · Meta-Analyses · Systematic Reviews |
| 3 | Randomized Controlled Trials |
| 4 | Prospective Cohort Studies |
| 5 | Case-Control Studies |
| 6 | Cross-Sectional Studies |
| 7 | Mechanistic Studies · Animal Studies · Laboratory Studies |
| 8 | Expert Opinion |
| 9 | Marketing Claims · Influencer Claims · Anecdotal Reports |

**Rules for use:**

- Every factual claim on the site should be traceable to a tier. If a claim can only be supported at Tier 7 or below (mechanistic/animal/expert opinion), the page must say so explicitly rather than presenting it with the confidence of a Tier 1–3 claim. Example phrasing pattern: *"This effect has been shown in laboratory studies; human clinical evidence is currently limited."*
- Tier 9 sources (marketing, influencer, anecdote) are never cited as evidence *for* an effect. They may be referenced only when the page is explicitly describing a claim in order to evaluate or debunk it (relevant on Myths vs Facts, Device Therapy, Products Guide).
- A single high-tier source outweighs many low-tier sources. The site does not "average" evidence quality — three Tier 8 opinions do not add up to one RCT.
- Tier alone doesn't guarantee relevance: a Tier 1 guideline about general skin cancer prevention isn't automatically the strongest source for a claim about cosmetic wrinkle reduction. Tier and topical fit are both required.

## 2. Handling Conflicting Evidence

Conflicting evidence is common in this domain (especially supplements, devices, and topical actives) and will be handled with a consistent decision process rather than cherry-picking the result that fits the page's narrative:

1. **Check tier first.** If a Tier 1–2 source conflicts with a lower-tier source, the higher tier governs the headline claim, but the lower-tier finding can still be mentioned as a caveat or area of active research.
2. **Check recency within the same tier.** Among comparable-tier sources, more recent systematic reviews/meta-analyses generally supersede older ones — but only if the newer review has comparable or better methodology, not by recency alone.
3. **Check population/dose fit.** A lot of apparent "conflict" in this literature is actually a population or dose mismatch (e.g., a supplement trial in elderly deficient subjects vs. healthy young adults). Where this is the actual explanation, the page states that explicitly instead of presenting it as unresolved controversy.
4. **If genuinely unresolved**, the page says so in plain language — e.g., *"Evidence on X is mixed: some trials show a benefit, others show none, and it isn't yet clear why."* This is preferred over false resolution in either direction (overstating benefit OR overstating skepticism).
5. **Never silently pick a side.** If the board's page draft leans toward one interpretation, the Research Gaps subsection (required on every page per Phase 4) must name the conflicting evidence that was de-emphasized and why.

## 3. Assessing Study Quality

Beyond tier, every study type considered for citation is screened against:

- **Sample size and power** — underpowered studies (common in small device/cosmetic trials) get flagged as preliminary regardless of tier.
- **Study duration** — many cosmetic studies run 8–12 weeks; claims about long-term (1+ year) outcomes from short studies are flagged as extrapolation, not direct evidence.
- **Blinding and control quality** — open-label cosmetic studies (very common in industry-funded device/skincare research) are treated as weaker than their nominal tier would suggest, and this is noted.
- **Outcome measure** — objective measurement (e.g., corneometer, validated imaging, biopsy histology) is weighted above subjective self-assessment or investigator visual grading alone, which is extremely common in this literature and a major source of inflated claims.
- **Replication** — a single trial, even an RCT, is treated more cautiously than a finding replicated across multiple independent trials.

---

## 4. Publication Bias

- The site assumes published cosmetic/skincare literature skews positive, since null results are less likely to be published and less likely to be funded in the first place.
- Where a claim rests on a small number of published trials with no apparent negative/null studies in a well-studied area, this absence is treated as a yellow flag, not as confirmation, and noted in Research Gaps.
- Preprints and trial registries are not used as primary support for claims on this site (too unvetted for a consumer education resource), but registry data (e.g., ClinicalTrials.gov) can be referenced to note when a relevant trial exists but hasn't reported results — this is a legitimate way to flag an evidence gap honestly.

---

## 5. Funding Bias & Industry Sponsorship

- Funding source is checked and disclosed in the page's citation/reference list (not necessarily in the body text for every single citation, but available) whenever the cited study is sponsored by a manufacturer of the product/device/ingredient being evaluated.
- Industry-funded studies are not excluded — excluding them entirely would remove a large share of the device and skincare literature — but industry funding is treated as a downgrade factor, especially when combined with other weaknesses (small sample, open-label, subjective outcomes, short duration). A well-designed, large, independently-replicated industry-funded RCT is still good evidence; a small open-label industry-funded study with subjective grading is not, regardless of its nominal "RCT" label.
- Where the only available evidence for a popular product/device is industry-funded, the page says so plainly: *"Independent (non-industry-funded) research on this specific product is currently limited."*

---

## 6. Sample Diversity

- Skin tone, age range, sex, and ancestry of study populations are noted when they materially affect whether a finding generalizes (this is especially relevant for pigmentation treatments, where much foundational research has historically skewed toward lighter Fitzpatrick types).
- Where a treatment's evidence base is concentrated in a narrow population, the page states this explicitly rather than implying universal applicability — directly relevant to Hyperpigmentation and Age/Genetics/Skin Type pages.
- This is a description of the literature's limits, not a claim about biological difference between groups — consistent with Phase 1's anti-stereotyping framing for the Genetics & Skin Type page.

---

## 7. Clinical Significance vs. Statistical Significance

- A statistically significant result (p < 0.05) is not automatically reported as meaningful. Wherever possible, the page reports the actual effect size (e.g., "a 12% reduction in wrinkle depth measured by silicone replica analysis") rather than just "statistically significant improvement."
- Where a study only reports significance without effect size, or where the effect size is small enough that it's unlikely to be noticeable to the user themselves, the page says so — e.g., *"While statistically significant, the average improvement was small enough that most users may not notice a visible difference."*
- This distinction is mandatory on Nutrition & Supplements, Skincare Ingredients, and Cost vs Benefit Calculator, where "statistically significant" claims are most commonly used to imply a bigger effect than exists.

---

## 8. Absolute vs. Relative Risk

- Any claim phrased as relative change ("reduces risk by 40%") is, wherever the source allows, also given in absolute terms ("from 2% to 1.2%"), since relative-risk framing alone is a common source of consumer-facing exaggeration.
- This applies most directly to Lifestyle (sun exposure/skin cancer framing) and any longevity-adjacent claims, where relative-risk language is common in both marketing and, honestly, in some press coverage of real studies.

---

## 9. Human Studies vs. Animal Studies

- Animal and in-vitro studies are cited only at Tier 7 and are explicitly labeled as such in-text — never folded into a sentence that implies human evidence. Pattern: *"In animal studies, X showed [effect]. Human trials are limited / ongoing / show a smaller effect."*
- Mechanistic plausibility from animal/lab studies is allowed to inform the Mechanism subsection of a page (explaining *why* something might work) but is not allowed to stand in for the Evidence subsection's claim about whether it *does* work in people.
- This distinction is especially load-bearing on Nutrition & Supplements and parts of Skincare Ingredients (e.g., many novel peptides and "exosome" claims currently rest mostly on preclinical data).

---

## 10. Mechanistic Studies vs. Clinical Outcomes

- Plausible mechanism is necessary but not sufficient. A page does not present "X increases collagen synthesis in cultured fibroblasts" as equivalent to "X visibly reduces wrinkles in people." Both can be true to report, but they answer different questions and are kept in their respective subsections (Mechanism vs. Evidence) rather than blended into one claim.
- Where a popular ingredient/device has strong mechanistic rationale but thin clinical outcome data (common with many "exosome," peptide, and at-home device claims), the page states the gap directly rather than letting the mechanism section's plausibility carry an implied clinical promise.

---

## 11. Formal Evidence Rating System

Every intervention (ingredient, supplement, device, procedure, lifestyle factor) reviewed on the site receives ratings across the following axes. Not all axes apply to every intervention type (e.g., "Cost-Effectiveness" doesn't meaningfully apply to "get more sleep"), but the rating is built consistently wherever it does apply.

### 11a. Evidence Quality (★ scale, drives the headline rating used across the site)

| Rating | Criteria |
|--------|----------|
| ★★★★★ Strong Evidence | Tier 1–2 support (guideline-level or meta-analysis/systematic review), generally consistent across multiple independent, adequately powered studies. |
| ★★★★ Moderate Evidence | Multiple Tier 3 RCTs, generally consistent, reasonable sample sizes, may have some industry funding but independently replicated. |
| ★★★ Emerging Evidence | A small number of Tier 3 RCTs or good Tier 4 cohort data; promising but not yet replicated widely, or mixed results across studies. |
| ★★ Weak Evidence | Mostly Tier 5–7 (case-control, cross-sectional, mechanistic/animal/lab); human clinical data sparse or absent. |
| ★ Minimal Evidence | Tier 8–9 only (expert opinion, marketing, anecdote), or actively contradicted by what limited human evidence exists. |

This is the rating shown as the headline ★ score throughout the site (per Phase 1's spec) and is always the *Evidence Quality* axis specifically — never a blend with effectiveness, safety, or cost. Those are reported as separate, adjacent ratings so a reader can see, for example, "strong evidence that this has a small effect" rather than the two being collapsed into one number.

### 11b. Magnitude of Effect

Qualitative band, paired with the actual effect size in plain language wherever the underlying data supports it:

- **Large** — Visible to most users within the stated timeline, supported by objective measurement.
- **Moderate** — Visible to many users, may take longer or require sustained use.
- **Small** — Statistically measurable, may not be visibly noticeable to the user themselves.
- **Negligible/Unclear** — Effect, if present, is not reliably distinguishable from no treatment.

### 11c. Safety

- **Generally well-tolerated** — Rare/mild side effects in studied populations.
- **Moderate caution** — Known, manageable side effects; specific populations should avoid (pregnancy, certain skin types, certain medications).
- **Significant caution** — Meaningful risk of adverse effects, requires professional supervision (most Cosmetic Procedures entries).

Safety ratings always cross-reference the page's "Who Should Avoid" subsection rather than standing alone.

### 11d. Cost-Effectiveness

- Reported as a relative band (Low / Moderate / High annual cost) plus a qualitative ROI note that explicitly combines cost with the Magnitude of Effect and Evidence Quality ratings above — this is the actual mechanism feeding the Cost vs Benefit Calculator (Page 15).

### 11e. Consistency of Evidence

- **Highly consistent** — Findings replicate across most independent studies.
- **Mostly consistent** — Majority of studies agree, some conflicting findings exist (see Section 2 above for how conflicts are resolved/disclosed).
- **Inconsistent** — Findings are genuinely mixed with no clear resolution yet.

### 11f. Long-Term Data

- **Available** — Studies extend 1+ years.
- **Limited** — Most studies are under 6 months; long-term outcome is not well established.
- **Absent** — No meaningful long-term data exists; explicitly noted wherever this is the case, especially relevant for newer devices and ingredients (exosomes, growth factors, newer peptides).

### 11g. Clinical Recommendation

A plain-language synthesis sentence combining the above axes — this is what populates each page's required "Clinical Bottom Line" subsection (Phase 4). Pattern: *"[Intervention] has [Evidence Quality] supporting a [Magnitude] effect on [outcome], is [Safety band], costs [Cost band] annually, and [Long-Term Data status]. Best suited for [Who Benefits]; not recommended for [Who Should Avoid]."*

## 12. How This Framework Plugs Into Phase 4

Every page's "Evidence" subsection will state the Tier(s) of source(s) used. Every intervention table (ingredients, supplements, devices, procedures, products) will carry the seven-axis rating from Section 11. Every "Clinical Bottom Line" is a direct synthesis of those ratings, not a freestanding editorial opinion.
